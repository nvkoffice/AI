Over the past several weeks, I’ve been refining an Enterprise AI Reference Architecture and have now structured it across three distinct layers: Solution Implementation, AI as a Capability Platform, and Enterprise AI Foundation. The goal is to separate concerns clearly — application logic, AI platform services, and enterprise-grade governance — rather than blending everything into a single diagram.

At the Solution Implementation Layer, the focus is business workflow execution. A user interacts through a FastAPI gateway into an AI orchestrator and intent router, which dynamically dispatches requests to specialized services: RAG for policy queries, MCP clients for secure system reads, ML forecasting engines for predictive scenarios, writeback agents for transactional updates, or direct LLM services for general chat. High-risk operations flow through approval/HITL validation before writeback. Context assembly, final LLM synthesis, guardrails, telemetry, and response handling complete the loop. This layer is deliberately modular: prediction engines, vector stores, MCP servers, and approval systems remain independently evolvable components. It represents “AI applied to business.”

The second layer, AI as a Capability Platform, abstracts common AI services into reusable infrastructure. Here we see the agentic runtime with a chief orchestrator coordinating domain agents, semantic caching, model routing, guardrails, observability, and FinOps controls. Model registry, alignment pipelines, drift tracking, and event streaming form the operational backbone. This layer treats AI as an internal platform capability — similar to how enterprises treat cloud platforms or DevOps toolchains — ensuring reuse, governance, and scalability across multiple solutions.

The third layer, Enterprise AI Foundation, embeds security, identity, compliance, auditability, and financial accountability. Enterprise RBAC integration, policy enforcement, event backbone (Kafka-style pub/sub), immutable audit trails, centralized logging, and cost attribution engines ensure that AI does not operate as an experimental silo but as governed enterprise infrastructure. Human-in-the-loop mechanisms and low-confidence routing enforce bounded autonomy, particularly in regulated environments.

By separating these three layers, the architecture achieves clarity: business solutions evolve independently of AI platform mechanics, and both remain governed by enterprise standards. It aligns AI with established enterprise architecture principles — layered abstraction, event-driven integration, identity-first security, policy enforcement outside the model boundary, and cost transparency through FinOps.
